Notes

Things I keep thinking about.

A small space for observations on trust, creator strategy, storytelling, and what changes when technology moves faster than language.

These are not polished manifestos. They are working thoughts. I like keeping them close to the rest of the portfolio because they say as much about how I think as any case study does.

Spring 2026 / Trust

Trust is becoming the real growth lever.

A lot of marketing still behaves like reach is the whole game. Reach matters, of course, but it has become much easier to manufacture surface-level attention than it is to build durable credibility.

The brands I admire most tend to feel legible. You understand what they care about, how they speak, and what kind of experience they are trying to create. That clarity makes people more likely to listen, and listening is still the starting point for any meaningful kind of growth.

I think this matters even more in categories where the underlying product or service asks people for belief. Premium brands, travel brands, and technology brands all live here in different ways. The work has to do more than look good. It has to make people feel they are in capable hands.

Spring 2026 / Creator strategy

Creator programs should feel more like communities and less like media buys.

The best creator partnerships rarely feel like one-off transactions. They feel like a brand has found people who can naturally carry its tone into the world.

That changes the job. It becomes less about squeezing a deliverable out of a brief and more about building an ecosystem that people actually want to stay part of. That means better selection, better brief writing, stronger feedback loops, and a more honest understanding of what each creator brings to the table.

I’m always interested in the difference between a campaign that technically worked and a program that creators genuinely want to keep showing up for. The second one is much harder to build, but it almost always compounds better over time.

Spring 2026 / Technology

Technology brands need better briefs, not just louder campaigns.

When a category is new, fast-moving, or even slightly intimidating, the brief matters more than people think. The brief determines how clearly a creator can explain the value, what tone they are allowed to take, and whether the final story feels like it came from a real human perspective.

I’m especially interested in how emerging technology brands show up in public. The temptation is often to be louder, faster, and more obviously future-facing. But audiences usually respond better when the story makes the technology feel legible, useful, and emotionally understandable.

The future is not only a product challenge. It is a communication challenge. Brands that solve that well are the ones that will feel genuinely modern instead of merely current.
